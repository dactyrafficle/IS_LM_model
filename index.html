the interest rate, blanchard 292+

  • The real interest rate is what connect goods today, x_t, to goods tomorrow, x_{t+1}

  • x_{t+1} = x_t(1+r_t)

  • Suppose you have x things.
  • The price for one unit of x_t is p_t. So you have m_t = x_tp_t dollars.
  • You invest the money at interest rate i_t
  • Next year, you have xp_t(1+i_t)
  • You use that money to buy x. But the price has changed, and is now p_{t+1}

  • So you can buy x_{t+1} = \dfrac{x_tp_t(1+i_t)}{p_{t+1}}

  • So at t you had x, but at t+1 you can have x_{t+1}

  • That means the real interest rate is x_t(1+r_t) = x_{t+1}

  • 1+r_t = (1+i_t) \cdot \dfrac{p_t}{p_{t+1}}

  • But we don't know what p_{t+1} until t+1, so we have to use E[p_{t+1}]

1+r_t = (1+i_t) \cdot \dfrac{p_t}{E[p_{t+1}]}

\pi_{t+1} = \dfrac{p_{t+1}}{p_t}

1+r_t = (1+i_t) \cdot ( 1 + E[ \pi_{t+1} ] ) ^{-1}

r_t \approx i_t - E[\pi_{t+1}]

idea behind the uncovered interest partity, blanchard p115

you can invest $1 today domestically at the domestic rate, and get (1+i) in one year

or you can take that $1, buy $e ($1 x e foreign/domestic) foreign currency, get i+i* interest yielding e(i+i*), but then you have to sell that back to domestic currency at e_{t+1}, so you get $$\dfrac{e_t(1+i^*)}{e_{t+1}}$$. But you don't know what next year's exchange rate will be so you guess.

$$\dfrac{e_t(1+i^*)}{E[e_{t+1}]}$$

Anyhow, those are your 2 options:

  • buy domestic bonds

  • buy foreign bonds

Comment on elasticities

y=x^a

\dfrac{\Delta y/y}{\Delta x/x} = \dfrac{\Delta y}{\Delta x} \cdot \dfrac{x}{y}

\dfrac{dy}{dx}=ax^{a-1}=\dfrac{ax^a}{x}=\dfrac{ay}{x}

\dfrac{\Delta y/y}{\Delta x/x} = \dfrac{\Delta y}{\Delta x} \cdot \dfrac{x}{y} = \dfrac{dy}{dx} \cdot \dfrac{x}{y} = \dfrac{ay}{x} \cdot \dfrac{x}{y} = a

The x-elasticity of y is a.

\dfrac{\Delta y/y}{\Delta x/x} = a

If \frac{\Delta x}{x} = 0.01, then \dfrac{\Delta y/y}{0.01} = a so that \frac{\Delta y}{y} = a \cdot 0.01

So a 1% change in x makes an a% change in y.
