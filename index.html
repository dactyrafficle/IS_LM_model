the interest rate, blanchard 292+

  • The real interest rate is what connect goods today, x_t, to goods tomorrow, x_{t+1}

  • x_{t+1} = x_t(1+r_t)

  • Suppose you have x things.
  • The price for one unit of x_t is p_t. So you have m_t = x_tp_t dollars.
  • You invest the money at interest rate i_t
  • Next year, you have xp_t(1+i_t)
  • You use that money to buy x. But the price has changed, and is now p_{t+1}

  • So you can buy x_{t+1} = \dfrac{x_tp_t(1+i_t)}{p_{t+1}}

  • So at t you had x, but at t+1 you can have x_{t+1}

  • That means the real interest rate is x_t(1+r_t) = x_{t+1}

  • 1+r_t = (1+i_t) \cdot \dfrac{p_t}{p_{t+1}}

  • But we don't know what p_{t+1} until t+1, so we have to use E[p_{t+1}]

1+r_t = (1+i_t) \cdot \dfrac{p_t}{E[p_{t+1}]}

\pi_{t+1} = \dfrac{p_{t+1}}{p_t}

1+r_t = (1+i_t) \cdot ( 1 + E[ \pi_{t+1} ] ) ^{-1}

r_t \approx i_t - E[\pi_{t+1}]

What is epsilon? And what is theta?

If L = A Y^{\epsilon}, then \frac{dL}{dY} = \epsilon A Y^{\epsilon-1} = \epsilon A Y^{\epsilon} Y^{-1} = \epsilon L Y^{-1}

So that means \dfrac{dL/L}{dY/Y} = \frac{dL}{dY} \frac{Y}{L} = \epsilon L Y^{-1} \cdot Y L^{-1} = \epsilon

If Y_2=1.01 \cdot Y_1, then \Delta Y = Y_2-Y_1=0.01Y_1

So \dfrac{\Delta L/L}{\Delta Y/Y} = \dfrac{\Delta L/L}{\Delta 0.01 \cdot Y_1/Y_1} = \dfrac{\Delta L/L}{0.01} = \epsilon

Meaning \Delta L/L = 0.01 \cdot \epsilon

So a 1% change in Y means an ε % change in L

Conversely, a 1% change in r means a -θ % change in L
